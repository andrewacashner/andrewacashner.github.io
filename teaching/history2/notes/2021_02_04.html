Changes of style

Palestrina: Basic elements/ingredients of style

  • all voices same words, different times
  • voices follow same melodic pattern with variations
    • imitative counterpoint – polyphony
      • fugue is a stricter version of this technique
  • structure: points of imitation – new melody in imitation for each phrase
  • strict control of consonance and dissonance
    • all strong beats are perfect (8s, 5s) or imperfect consonances (6s, 3s); cadences 8/5/3, 8/5 triads
    • dissonances are always prepared by step, and resolve down by step
  • duple meter, alla breve time – common for Latin-texted liturgical music
  • rhythm? opening: breve, semibreve, sb, minim, mn dotted, semiminim
    • connected to idea, feeling of the words
    • words: Psalm 42: “As the deer pants/longs after streams of water, so my soul longs for you, O Lord”
  • word painting (“madrigalisms”)
    • text projection - making the words clear
    • text depiction - painting/illustrating, symbolism, representation
    • text expression - style, genre, conventions that bring out the feeling, amplify the experience of the text

In sum

  • This motet is the paradigm of a style later known as stile antico (Old style), or stylus ecclesiasticus (church style)
  • Palestrina’s music (along with similar music by Francisco Guerrero, Orlando di Lasso, and others) was widely performed through the 18th century and served as a paradigm (model, standard) of the old-fashioned, traditional way of making music
  • Examples:
    • J. S. Bach regularly had his choir perform Palestrina in 18th-century Leipzig
    • This style is the basis of Johann Joseph Fux, Gradus ad parnassum (18C counterpoint treatise)
      • used by Haydn, Mozart; Salieri used it to teach Schubert and Liszt;
      • Nadia Boulanger used principles derived from it to teach Aaron Copland, Virgil Thomson, Quincy Jones, many others in 20th cent.
  • European missionaries saw this style as proof of their cultural superiority, similar to one-point perspective in visual art; they describe other cultures’ music as “all on one note” (like 2D instead of 3D)

Aspects of “old world” worldview

  • earth-centered cosmos
  • church as center and source of knowledge
  • hierarchy (church, nobility) as God-given order
  • harmony between the lowly and heavenly, between mundane and otherworldly
    • Neoplatonism: earthly things are imperfect reflections of heavenly ideals
    • 2 : 1 octave, 3 : 2 fifth, 4 : 3 fourth, 5 : 4 major third

Changing elements moving into 17th century

  • new, Humanistic approach to text: representing and expressing human feelings
  • emphasis on realism, even breaking the traditional rules for expression

Vicente Lusitano

  • Black Portuguese composer, theorist
  • chromatic composition, daring, bold expression
  • using dissonance for expressive purposes

Claudio Monteverdi

  • debate with Artusi over madrigal “Cruda Amarylli” for breaking counterpoint rules
  • brother Guilio Cesare Monteverdi defends him by saying those rules describe the prima prattica (first practice), the traditional style; but they are following a new approach, a seconda prattica initiated earlier by composers like Rore, Willaert, and Gesualdo
  • “The words are the mistress (the noble lady in charge) of the music, not the servant.”
  • also says that he is just writing down things that they were already doing in practice (tension between oral and written traditions, and a reminder that we need to look at more than just the score)
