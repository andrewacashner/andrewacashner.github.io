Times and places

  • Times
    • liturgical worship
      • Mass (Sunday - centered the Eucharist)
        • Mass ordinary (Kyrie, Gloria, Credo, Sanctus, Agnus) – same words every time
        • Mass proper - unique to the day, reflects season (for example, Introit, Alleluia, Communion verse)
          • musical settings: motets
      • Divine Office (daily prayer - centered on Psalms)
        • Vespers: starts with five psalms and antiphons (Dixit Dominus, psalm 110)
        • Matins: evening before - long service

          • psalms

          • responsory chants
          • readings -> three Nocturnes

          • eight responsory chants + Te Deum

    • religious music outside formal liturgy
      • Saturday Salve services dedicated to Virgin Mary
      • litanies and processions - e.g., for rain, for peace in time of war, for end of plague
      • celebratory processions - e.g., Corpus Christi
        • floats sponsored by confraternities - religious clubs
        • Black Kings Festivals at Epiphany (Jan 6)
      • sites for contestations of power, demonstrations of identity (e.g., indigenous Andeans playing role of “Moors” in battles of Moors and Christians at Corpus Christi)
      • sites for social release and social control
    • the liturgical year
      • highlights:
        • celebrating life of Christ: Christmas & Epiphany (birth), Holy Week and Easter (death, resurrection), Corpus Christi (sacramental presence in the Eucharist)
        • celebrating life of Mary: Conception (later codified as “Immaculate Conception” – Mary conceived without sin in order to be Christ’s mother, not Christ’s conception); Anunciation (receiving message from angel that she would give birth to Jesus); Assumption (raised up bodily into heaven rather than dying)
        • celebrating lives of saints, varying by location: widely celebrated saints’ days included John the Baptist at midsummer, St. Nicholas near Christmas
  • Places
    • cathedral (has a bishop who sits in the cathedra/chair), parish churches (no bishop)
      • center of community life
    • convent
      • haven for women and hub of cultural production; some convents specialized in music and provided advanced training
    • plaza
      • processions, festivals, mixture of official and popular religion
    • home
    • mission
    • ship
      • need for portable, simple forms of singing and instruments
      • chant, hymns, falso bordone of different kinds (improvised polyphony, usually in chords)

Continuing traditions

  • chant
    • chants with antiphons (psalms)
    • alternatim practice (chant vs polyphony; choral vs organ)
  • falso bordone, improvised polyphony
    • fabordón (Spanish), faux bourdon (French), faburden (English)
    • cf. Anglican chant
    • example: Allegri, Miserere
    • canto de órgano (Spanish term for polyphony)
  • stile antico polyphony
    • Palestrina
    • Francisco Guerrero (chapelmaster at Seville Cathedral)

New and changing styles, genres

  • Styles
    • polychoral texture (example: Gabrieli)
    • monody, recitative (example: “Nigra sum” from Monteverdi, Vespers)
    • arias (comes in more later)
    • prevalent polystylism
  • Genres
    • oratorio
      • theatrical work on religious/Biblical theme, sponsored by Oratorio Society of St. Philip of Neri, held in “Oratorio” building, hence the name
      • often in vernacular languages and appealing more to common people; there are examples of recitative/monody style in oratorios even before the experiments in opera
    • sacred concerto (new style of motet)
      • Example: Viadana
      • Viadana’s approach: take a polyphonic motet, sing the melody, play the bass line, have the keyboard player or lutenist cover the other lines; in other words replace a polyphonic texture with a solo-and-accompaniment texture using figured bass
      • Give the feel of imitative counterpoint by having a single voice imitate itself at different intervals
      • Allowed for new kind of more individualistic expression in monody, but also versatile and practical in an age of upheaval, war
    • organ verset
      • improvised music introducing, or standing in place of, choral music
      • choir and organ could go back and forth (alternatim practice) on verses of a psalm or canticle like the Magnificat
      • variety of subtypes develop in different countries, for example
        • Italy:
          • toccata (intro, prelude)
          • toccata per l’Elevazione (mysterious music played while priest elevates Eucharistic wafer and people “adore with the eyes” because they don’t actually receive communion)
          • ricercar (“research” – like an imitative, old-style motet at the keyboard)
        • France
          • a whole system of types of versets develops, distinguished by which stops (registrations) were used in certain characteristic textures (e.g., tierce en taille, third in the Tenor)
        • Iberia (Spain, Portugal)
          • tientos (like toccatas)
          • tiento de falsas (featuring lots of musica ficta; like an elevation toccata?)
          • pieces featuring the common split keyboard, where you can play a solo voice in the right hand (like a “cornet” combination) with a different sound for the accompaniment in the left hand
          • batallas (musical battle scenes, why not? we’ll say it’s about fighting with the devil, but have lots of fun with trumpet fanfares and weird dissonances – beginnings of a tradition of battle imitation music that goes all the way to Beethoven, Mahler, and film music)
    • villancico
      • settings of vernacular poetry with a central estribillo (refrain), strophic coplas (verses), and repeat of all or part of estribillo
        • (sometimes other sections also like introduction or *responsión)
      • originally a genre of courtly love poetry sung in aristocratic homes; brought into church, especially for Christmas Matins, c. 1590–1600
      • composed in sets of eight or more to be performed in Matins (paired with or replacing Responsory chants); could also be performed at Mass and other times
      • typically polychoral and polystylistic, emphasis on clarity of text
      • some level of engagement with popular/folk poetry and music, but extent is still unknown
