Music on the Roman Catholic Missions

Types of missions, types of interactions with surrounding people

(Gauvin Bailey, Art on the Jesuit Missions)

  • Outer circle
    • minority surrounded by powerful political entities
    • interfacing with well-established “civilized” cultures
    • China (Macao), Japan (Nagasaki?), India (Goa)
    • Japanese word for them was “southern barbarians”
  • Inner circle
    • conquered indigenous peoples (post-war, post-pandemic, etc.)
    • dominant
    • colonization, civilizing mission
    • Mexico, Peru
  • Jesuits - Society of Jesus - founded by Ignatius of Loyola mid 16C
    • society of priests, military-style organization, answerable to pope
    • enforcers of Post-Tridentine Catholic church
    • desperate men trying to save their own souls
    • jacks of all trades
  • Missionary goals
    • Compare vs. Franciscans (outreach to poor)
    • Jesuits wanted to reach upper echelons of society, especially youth
    • impress people with art, music, and science
      • e.g., bringing organs, harpsichords, mechanical clocks to Chinese imperial court

Sites of missions in South and East Asia

  • Jesuits in Mughal India
    • succeeded in becoming dealers of religious for Mughal connoisseurs
  • In Japan:
    • succeeded in ministry to the poor
    • highly accommodationist
    • brutally repressed (Silence)
  • In China:
    • scientific/mathematical/musical/philosphical exchanges led by Matteo Ricci, accepted as resident philosopher in Chinese court
    • entertained as a curiosity but without much long-term impact

Concepts of intercultural exchange, encounter, conflict

  • Hybridity

  • “spaces of correlation” (Cécile Fromont)
    • example: Guaraní sculpture of Virgin Mary – European image vs. indigenous interest in textiles and geometrical patterns
    • example: for Jesuits in China – keyboard and tuning

  • incommensurability – two things where one cannot be measured with the same rule used to measure the other
