Essays

  • Communication is possible within a Community with shared Conventions

The Reformed

Communities and Leaders

  • Switzerland (Zwingli), France (Jean Calvin)
  • Scotland (John Knox)
    • Presbyterians - elders (presbyters) - representative government
      • vs. Episcopalians - bishops (episkopoi)

Attitude toward tradition and views on music

  • Calvin: “if it’s not commanded in Scripture it should be forbidden”
  • strip away, get back to bare essentials, start fresh
  • just the psalms from the Old Testament, sung monophonically in metrical verse
  • Genevan Psalter

Musical practices

  • Psalmody
    • “lined out” by precentor
  • “The Old Way of Singing”
    • quasi-improvised, ornamented, slow form of unaccompanied congregational singing
    • influenced other traditions
      • e.g, Native American Christian singing: Oneida Hymn Singers
      • African American Christian singing: Dr. Watts Singing (and then influencing many subsequent Black traditions)

Radicals

  • radical, want to get back to the radix or root
  • Quakers – worship in total silence

Anglicans

  • national church (episcopalian structure)
  • compromise

  • vernacular worship: Book of Common Prayer

  • Puritans were Calvinist/Presbyterian dissenters who fled to America in order to establish theocratic societies without having to compromise and get along with other kinds of Christians

Attitude toward tradition and views on music

Musical practices

  • Cathedral (choral/organ music) vs. parish (simple psalmody, later hymn singing)

  • Anthems (similar to sacred concertos or motets; vernacular, often psalm-based texts)
    • Types: “Verse” (solo or chamber ensemble) or “full” (what it sounds like)

  • Organ voluntaries: like versets

  • French influence later in 17C

Intercultural Encounter in New England

  • Massachusetts: Puritans and Wampanoags

  • New York: French Catholics, Dutch Reformed, English Protestants and the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois)

  • Characteristics of New England Puritan religious worldview led them to see themselves as the persecuted chosen people of God with a right to take over indigenous peoples’ land
    • e.g., saw smallpox epidemic they caused as evidence that God had “cleared the land” for them
