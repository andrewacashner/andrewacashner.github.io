Change in styles from “early” to “high” Baroque music

  • Late 17C Italian string music and the development of tonality

  • Opening contrast: Schütz vs. Vivaldi motets
    • technically sacred concerto vs. cantata

Schütz, Siehe, wie fein und lieblich ist (Ps. 133)

- duple
- two high strings, a low string, continuo, chorus
- phrase by phrase setting
- symbolism of relationship of strings to each other and to choir
- imitation, canon, fugue

Antonio Vivaldi, Nulla in mundo pax sincera (cantata)

- 12/8 (compound duple)
- two high strings, continuo, solo voice
- string intro and interludes and outro
    - *ritornello* - "the little thing that returns"
- A B A ( = da capo aria): written A B "da capo"
- continuous rhythmic movement, continuous bass movement
- some short melodic phrases, some very long ones
- *Fortspinnung* (spinning forth)
- groove-based music
- hint of imitation, melody and accompaniment

- harmonic sequences
- consistent patterns of large-scale harmonic planning that reinforce
  functional tonality (I -> V -> I on multiple levels)
- consistent affect/mood for the whole piece, and esp. for whole sections
    (internal contrast between A and B sections)
- comparatively virtuosic string writing, especially featuring string
  crossings

- all typical features of "High Baroque" style, an international style

Arcangelo Corelli, Opus 1 Sonatas da chiesa

  • Two violins and continuo
  • Used in Catholic worship in same places as organ music or motets
  • Follow some of same conventions as that music (e.g., “Ky- ri- e” openings followed by fugal section, like many choral Kyrie settings)
  • Harmonic sequences and tonal tonic–dominant motion

  • Relatively virtuosic string writing

  • Hugely popular, disseminated all over the world
    • e.g., performed at the imperial court in China
