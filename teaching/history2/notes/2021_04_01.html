St. Matthew Passion

Textures, elements

  • Opening chorus
    • Double orchestra (strings, winds, continuo), and chorus, SATB x2 plus soprano ripieno
  • Recitative
    • secco recitative (dry, unaccompanied)
    • extra strings whenever Jesus speaks (halo)
    • Continuo (organ + bass)
  • Chorale (hymn)
    • SATB homorhythmic setting of a pre-existing melody (harmonization)
    • doubled with instruments
  • Recitative/Aria pair
    • accompanied recitative as intro to aria
    • aria: solo song
      • ritornello - instrumental intro
      • da capo aria: A B A’

Cantatas

Outgrowth of motets –> sacred concertos –> “concerto with aria cantata” –> cantata

Multiple movements, recitative/aria pairs (like Catholic cantatas/cantadas in Spain)

Lutherans add chorales (hymn), choruses

Service: - monophonic congregational song - organ preludes/fugues - organ chorale preludes (intro to congregational song) - vocal/choral music: cantatas - performed in two halves, on either side of the sermon - responding to theme of the day, Gospel reading

Passion

  • Musical setting of the entire last portion of the gospel of Matthew
  • Passion = suffering
  • the story of the suffering and death of Jesus
  • sung in the liturgy, before and after the sermon, on Good Friday (Friday before Easter)
  • premiered in 1729 at St. Thomas Church (Thomaskirche) in Leipzig

Roles - Evangelist (tenor) - Jesus (bass) - Turba (crowd) - Pilate - Synagoga: Jewish leaders

  • action of the narrative - recitative with characters, turba chorus
  • reflection - arias, chorales, choruses

Opening chorus

  • double fugato
  • polychoral dialogue
  • chorale

Mass in B minor

  • anthology of reworkings of Bach’s personal “greatest hits”
  • like a last will and testament or personal monument
  • encyclopedic treatment of all possible kinds of church music

Confiteor/Et expecto resurrectionum from Creed

  • Old-style strict contrapuntal motet over modern High-Baroque running bass
  • becomes cantus firmus motet based on plainchant Creed melody, sung by two voices in canon
    • symbolism of canon as “law” and baptism as sign of faith and the creed as the “rule of faith”
  • turns very modern and highly chromatic for depiction of resurrection (decayed bodies being raised as glorified bodies for eternal life)

  • then gives way to more modern choral movement with trumpets (symbolic and expressive of joy), including a melismatic fugue (symbolic of eternity and heaven)

  • Bach’s goal in putting out music for posterity is always that you would “go and do likewise”
