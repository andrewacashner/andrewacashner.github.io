Identifying and naming “the new style”

  • Example, Gluck, “Che farò senza Eurydice?”
    • simple tonal harmony (I - IV - V - I)
    • non-continuous bass line
    • periodic phrasing
      • antecedent, consequent
      • hierarchical
    • strong–weak motion, tension and release
  • Wiener Klassik/Classical/Classic
    • Neoclassical? (art historical/philosophical sense of connection to Greece/Rome)
    • Classic? (historical sense of works that have stood the test of time as foundational reference works for later creators)
      • tied to a specific performance history and history of criticism
      • need to acknowledge the cultural assumptions and especially the framework/ideology of white cultural supremacy behind this
    • people at the time did not use these terms
    • false contrast with “Romantic”
      • first time Romantik was used, by ETA Hoffmann, referring to Mozart and Haydn
    1. 1730-1780: galant/Enlightenment (James Webster)
    1. 1780-1830, Viennese: Viennese modern

Analytical approaches

Worship

  • Charles Rosen, The Classical Style (c. 1975)
  • Carl Shachter: “we are here to understand the workings of great masterpieces of music”

Form

  • 19th century Formenlehre – “sonata–allegro form”
  • 20th–21st century formal analysis (Darcy/Hepokowski)

Harmony

  • Post-Rameau functional harmonic analysis (chords with roots, inversions)
  • Schenkerian analysis
    • stripping away “surface” level to find deep underlying structure of large-scale voice leading patterns, nearly always ^3--^2--^1
    • explicitly designed to demonstrate supremacy of Beethoven and other German geniuses
    • form, style, text, etc., are all “surface” level things and mostly ignored

Psychology

  • Expectation/surprise (Meyer)

  • connection to genre theory (Kallberg)
    • looking at a community’s expectations for communication in a particular type of music, an agreement between audience and creator about what will happen in that type of music

Style

  • Topic theory (Allanbrook, Monelle)
    • allusions to other pre-existing styles of music from “outside” the piece, within a single piece

Construction

  • Schemata theory (Gjerdingen)
    • reconstructed compositional practice of Neapolitan conservatory tradition
    • building up from simple voice-leading patterns
    • how Mozart and others actually learned to compose

Historic

  • Using descriptions of musical procedure and expectations from the actual time period
  • e.g., Koch tried to describe the pattern of pieces called sonatas in the 18th century
