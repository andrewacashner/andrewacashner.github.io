Margaret Butler, Italian Opera in the Eighteenth Century

(CH18CM) 203-217, 221-236

Reading questions

General

  • What is the primary focus of this chapter? Why did the author, Margaret Butler, decide not organize the chapter around composers and their works?
  • How are operas like suits of clothes?

  • The goal of reading this chapter should be to understand, at a basic level, how opera was created in the eighteenth century—the cities and institutions that fostered it, and how they did so; the types of opera performed in these places; and the main composers and the types of opera they composed—and to understand the main conventions of the most common opera types (serious opera, or opera seria, and comic opera, or opera buffa).

    The nitty-gritty details like the Italian names of theaters and dates of specific operas are less important than the big picture: focus on the main types of theaters, the most prominent cities, the main distinctions of different kinds of opera, and pay attention to things mentioned repeatedly.

Elements of the opera
  • What were the types and occasions for arias?

  • What is the form of a da capo aria? (Cf. Broschi, Son qual nave)
  • A suitcase aria is one that a star singer would carry from place to place and demand on performing in any opera (possibly without regard to the story or character). Son qual nave was the famous suitcase aria of the greatest castrato, Farinelli, composed for him by his brother and notated with the kind of ornamentation that Farinelli improvised.

  • The Querelle des bouffons (dispute of the buffoons or clowns) was a controversy in Paris about the relative merits of Italian comic opera compared to French operatic traditions. See the Rousseau reading on the syllabus.
  • (235) The Neapolitan conservatories were originally orphanages where the children were taught music both for their discipline and to raise money for the institution. The conservatory teachers developed ingenious methods for teaching musical performance and composition using memory devices and stock patterns, and the techniques and style developed in the conservatories came to dominate European music through the beginning of the nineteenth century.
