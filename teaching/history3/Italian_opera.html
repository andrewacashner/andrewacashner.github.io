Italian Opera in the Eighteenth Century

Riccardo Broschi, Son qual nave (1734)

  • Composed for Riccardo's brother Carlo, the famous castrato known as Farinelli
  • Suitcase aria interpolated into Artaserse with libretto by Metastasio
  • Historical account of this aria's performance, from A. Burgh, Anecdotes of Music, Historical and Biographical (London, 1814), Vol. III, Letter 62 (p. 89):
    In the year 1734, as we have already observed, he [Farinelli] came to England, where the effects which his surprising talents had upon the audience, were ecstasy! rapture! enchantment! In the famous air, Son qual Nave, which was composed for him by his brother, the first note he sung, was taken with such delicacy, swelled by minute degrees to such an amazing volume, and afterwards diminished in the same manner to a mere point, that it was applauded for full five minutes. After this he set off with such brilliancy, and rapidity of execution that it was difficult for the violins of those days to keep pace with him. In short, in comparison with all other singers, he was as superior as the famous horse Childers, to all other racers.

Text and Translation

Son qual nave ch'agitata
da più scogli in mezzo all'onde
si confonde e spaventata
va solcando in alto mar. (Fine)
Ma in veder l'amato lido
lascia l'onde, e il vento insido
e và in porto à riposar. (Da capo al Fine)

I am like a boat that, tossed
from the rocks in the midst of the waves,
confused and frightened,
plows on through the high sea.
But on seeing the blessed shore
leaves the waves, and the treacherous wind
goes into the harbor to rest.

Johann Adolf Hasse, Cleofide, Digli ch'io son fedele (1731)

  • Performed in Dresden
  • Libretto by Michelangelo Boccardi, adapted from Metastasio's Alessandro nell'Indie
  • See Peter Burkholder, ed., Norton Anthology of Western Music, 7th ed. (New York: Norton, 2014), vol. II, no. 108

Text and Translation

Digli ch'io son fedele
Digli ch'è il mio tesoro:
Che m'ami, ch'io l'adoro,
Che non disperi ancor. (Fine)

Digli che la mia stella
Spero placar col pianto;
Che lo consoli intanto
L'immagine di quella
Che vive nel suo cor. (D.C. al Fine)

Tell him that I am faithful,
tell him that he is my treasure,
tell him to love me, that I adore him,
tell him not to despair any more.

Tell him that I hope to appease
my star with weaping,
that meanwhile, may he be consoled
by the image of the one
who lives in his heart.

Giovanni Battista Pergolesi, La serva padrona (1733), Son imbrogliato io già

  • Performed at the Teatro San Bartolomeo in Naples; then to great acclaim all over Europe, especially Paris
  • Libretto by G. A. Federico
  • Originally performed as an intermezzo between the acts of an opera seria by Pergolesi

Serpina (To herself) Have it your way for now – in the end, things will turn out my way.

Serpina Want to see my husband?

Serpina I am going to send for him; He's waiting down in the street.
Uberto Go.
Serpina With your permission. (Serpina leaves)
Uberto Now let's guess who this man will be!
