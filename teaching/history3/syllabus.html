Learning Goals and Assessments

Course Description

Catalog Description

The history of western art music from approximately 1730–1850, with an emphasis on analysis of the masterpieces of tonal music and their relationship to society and other arts. Lectures with extensive listening, reading, and analysis.

Full Description

This course, as part of a four-semester sequence of courses in the history of Western music for music majors, will give you the opportunity to engage in the close study of practices of music-making from around 1730 to 1850 in Europe and its colonies. Our primary goal is to deepen our understanding of music by learning to think about music historically. This means that we want to understand how musical practices have changed over time, and how music fits into a broader history of culture. We will pursue a holistic view of music history through a dialectic approach that moves back and forth between sonic and social aspects of music. By building broad knowledge of representative examples of music composed and performed in this period across multiple social settings (opera house, church, home, concert hall), and through in-depth analysis of a smaller repertoire, you will develop a conceptual framework for making sense of music from 1730 to 1850 in a way that is relevant to contemporary concerns. Our emphasis is on the complex music patronized by the upper classes of Europe, which they came to regard as part of a canon of classic masterworks; but we will consider also oral and mass-mediated popular traditions, and we will pay attention to the musical creativity of women, people of color, and disadvantaged groups. The course will equip you to make your own decisions about how to tell the story of music and to help write the next chapter through your own historically-informed music-making.

Learning Goals

  1. Build a conceptual framework for understanding musical practices in both sonic and social aspects, with emphasis on notated music from Europe and its colonies, approximately 1730 to 1850. (Know the music, know the world around the music.)
  2. Develop a critical perspective on the scholarly and ethical problems of creating music history. (Know how the story has been told and why; reflect on how it should be told.)
  3. Develop skills in the analysis of musical forms and styles, including large orchestral works. This includes developing facility in score reading and engaging with music through performance. (Be able to engage with the music in depth and know how it works.)
  4. Develop skills in historical research, writing, oral communication, and collaboration. (Know where to find more information; be able to synthesize information and communicate an argument clearly and persuasively; know how to engage with other people’s ideas productively, including facing difficult topics.)

Essays

Scholarly article review

Goal: Understand how musicologists contribute to scholarly discourse on music by using evidence to advance an argument. Gain a heightened sense of what kinds of questions scholars are asking and how they pursue the answers.

Choose any research article published after 2015, and dealing with historical aspects of music between 1730–1850, in one of the following journals or another approved by the professor:

  • Journal of Musicology
  • Journal of the American Musicological Society
  • Acta musicologica
  • Eighteenth-Century Music
  • Nineteenth-Century Music
  • Opera Quarterly
Write a detailed summary of the article’s argument and analyze how the author uses evidence to support this argument. Appraise the strengths and weaknesses of the argument and its presentation. Suggest avenues for further research that could build on or challenge this article.

4–5 pages (one-inch margins, 12-pt. font, double spaced), about 1500 words

See schedule for due date

Analytical/historical essay: Symphonies in sound and society

Goal: Understand the dynamics between sonic and social aspects of music as they pertain to changes in the genre of symphony over time. Develop skills in close reading/listening and in analytical and historical writing. Use evidence to construct a persuasive argument. Revise and improve your writing based on feedback.

Select one symphony composed between 1730 and 1780, and another from 1780 to 1830. Compare and contrast how the composers use the conventions of the symphony genre and the sonata–allegro form especially. Connect their musical technique to their social setting, drawing on primary and secondary sources. Examples of primary sources include the Strunk Source Readings, letters by the composer, reviews of early performances, diary entries. Examples of secondary sources include scholarly articles and books about the social world of symphonies in this period.

Your essay should include a general analysis of two complete symphonies, and a detailed analysis of two first movements in sonata–alegro form. Your analysis should not attempt to account for every aspect of the piece or go bar-by-bar through the score; instead you should focus on certain problems in the music related to your argument (e.g., harmonic movement in the slow introductions, motivic development in the retransition, music topics like learned style or horn calls).

The purpose of the essay is not to demonstrate that later symphonies are better than earlier ones, but to understand how and why the genre changed.

7–8 pages, about 2200 words

See schedule for due date

Learning Reflections

Goal: For you, to periodically reflect on your own learning process, identify milestones, challenges, and areas of growth. For the professor, to assess your engagement in active learning, to get to know each student, to gain a better sense of what intellectual themes are emerging in the class, what students are retaining and what is challenging for them.

Write three short (1–2 pp.) essays in which you reflect on your learning process. What have been the most significant new perspectives you’ve gained since your last essay? What was most challenging to your preconceived notions or previous knowledge? What was your favorite/least favorite music and why? What challenges have you faced in learning? What about the class has helped or hindered your efforts to learn?

Due dates on schedule

Grading is based on the clarity of the writing, the depth of engagement with course materials, the degree of critical reflection on your own learning shown in the text. Grading is not based on any opinions you express about the course content or the class.

Group Performance Project

Goal: Gain hands-on knowledge of musical craft through arrangement and performance. Develop skills in collaboration, oral presentation, and performance. Develop administration, entrepeneurial, and communication skills relevant to music careers.

Working together with 1–3 other students, arrange, prepare a brief presentation, and perform any type of music studied in the course. Arrange and present the work in a way that would highlight its relevance and interest to contemporary audiences.

Due date on schedule

Credit Hours and Workload

This course fulfills university requirements for five credit hours. The class meets for two sessions per week with the professor, each containing 90 minutes of instructional time. The class meets for one workshop session per week with the teaching assistant, for 50 minutes of instructional time. Students are expected to do about 30 minutes of work outside of class in addition to the normal time expected for study and assignments. Normally this will consist of work on the group project and individual writing assignments.

These are meant to be hands-on learning opportunities to build your skills and give you the chance to get help in learning; as well as allowing us to deal with some topics that don’t fit as well in the format of the rest of the class.

Academic Integrity

All assignments and activities associated with this course must be performed in accordance with the University of Rochester’s Academic Honesty Policy.

Academic honesty means avoiding anything that would unfairly advance your academic standing over that of your classmates, such as cheating on an exam or presenting someone else’s work as your own. Plagiarism includes any verbatim copying; unattributed, incorrectly attributed, false, or misleading citations; or unacknowledged help from others (e.g., having someone else write a paper for you).

Group assignments will include specific additional instructions about how to comply with the Academic Honesty Policy in your collaborative work. If you are not sure whether something counts as plagiarism or academic dishonesty, I can help you if you ask me about it before turning in the assignment.

Every instance of academic dishonesty will be reported to the College’s Board on Academic Honesty. The typical penalty for plagiarism on an assignment is a grade of zero in the assignment and a one-third reduction in the total letter grade for the course. Penalties for repeat or egregious offenses can extend to a grade of XE in the course or suspension from the university. Please note that plagiarism on a draft is typically treated as a separate infraction from plagiarism on a final revision of the same paper.
