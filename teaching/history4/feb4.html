Nationalism, conclusion from last week

Key Concepts

  1. Self vs. Other; Unmarked (default) vs. Marked categories
  2. Imagined communities (from Benedict Anderson):

    Anderson argues that vernacular print media helped shape new concepts of national communities that though imaginary had real impact in the world (e.g., WWI); how would music fit into this theory? How would public spaces become sites for imaginary communities to form (e.g., opera house, symphony hall)?

  3. Unmarked (universal) style in late 19th cent. (e.g., orchestral style and formal techniques of middle Beethoven, Schubert, Mendelssohn, R. Schumann, etc.)
  4. Various styles marked as Other: Exoticism, musical nationalism

Exoticism

Often-generic/stereotyped Other-sounding music, defining the Self by reference to an imaginary Other

Example: Saint-SÃ¤ens Bacchanale. Take each element of music and Other it in some way.

Musical nationalism

Intentionally marking your own music as Other (with regard to the international default) in order to distinguish music as having a national character, especially deriving from an imagined Volksgeist (spirit of the Folk, based on musical and cultural sources closer to peasant culture and therefore to the land). Creating a conception of Self as Other. The result is something perceived as National at home (Self as Other in a good way), and marketable as exotic abroad.

Dvorak in Europe

Example: Dvorak, Slavonic Dances for four hands (big market success, promoted by Brahms to his own publisher, put Dvorak on the map)

Dvorak, Dumky Trio middle movement. What is similar here to normal chamber music of the time (cf. Brahms Cello Sonata from prev. week)? What is different? Dumky was a Czech dance type that alternated slow and fast sections. Look at elements of music to see how they have been Othered in the different sections, and to what degree? What are the social and economic constraints on how far Dvorak can go to make this sound Czech? (E.g., think about how someone like Amy Fay or C.V. Stanford would hear this.)

Mussorgsky Boris Godunov.

The nationalist opera to end all nationalist operas. Based on Russian history, in Russian language, with musical style based on Russian language and folk sources.

Mussorgsky was one of a group of Russian nationalist composers (The Mighty Handful) but went much further than the others to radically remake the sound of music along Russian lines. The opening of the opera is based on (1) the sound of bells in Russian coronation/church ceremonial, (2) a tritone ostinato that divides the octave equally and thereby produces a sense of stasis (does it also symbolize evil or create an affect of foreboding/dread/doom?), (3) the style and (I think) actual quotations of Russian Orthodox church music, which is exclusively choral with no instruments, (4) a style for the vocal soloists that aims to match Russian inflection and speech-rhythm perfectly, and aims to continue after Wagner in the idea of an opera without artifice (i.e., no arias, recitatives). The whole stage spectacle presented to Russians an nationalist image of themselves and to the rest of the world an image of exotic faraway (primitive, etc.) Russia.

Mussorgsky was an alcoholic and died leaving many compositions unfinished. His colleage Nicolai Rimsky-Korsakov (also very important figure in music education in Russia, look it up) finished the works for him. What this really meant, though, was that Rimsky Europeanized many of the most radical Slavic elements to make the works more palatable to a broader audience. He gave them a glossy, professional orchestration and smoothed over the rough patches (which were, arguably, meant to be rough). (Dmitri Shostakovich would later try his own completions that aimed to stay closer to Mussorgsky's vision.) Consider again: What are the social and economic constraints on how nationalistic you could get in Othering your own music?

Compare to very opening of Pictures at an Exhibition. Think again about elements of music. How many are altered here? (scale, meters, modal harmony, etc.) But then note the expression marking: Nel modo russico. What does this assume? That there is such a thing as Russian style; and that the best way to convey this idea to music readers is to express it in Italian. Good example of the challenge of being different and remaining intelligible at the same time.

Dvorak in America

Look up the history of the New World Symphony, and Dvorak's visit to America (New York and Spillville, Iowa) courtesy of J. Thurber, director of American Conservatory. Discuss Dvorak's article on American music and his proposed method for developing a national music. Does this fit with our theory of musical nationalism (think especially about Othering various elements of music).

Sources: Dvorak read about Native American music in a newspaper article, and had African-American conservatory student Harry T. Burleigh (look into his subsequent composition career) sing Negro spirituals for him. The more important source, I would argue, was European fantasy conceptions of America, such as those promulgated by James Fennimore Cooper's novels (Last of the Mohicans) and German artist Albert Bierstadt's paintings (do an image search). Dvorak specifically mentioned H. W. Longfellow's poem Song of Hiawatha as an inspiration (look up, cf. S. Coleridge-Taylor's cantata based on same text). This was a hugely popular fantasy of Native folklore with some real sources but primarily invented by the author.

We do not know for sure but there is reason to think the slow movement of the New World symphony is based on Hiawatha's funeral in Longfellow's poem. Go through the different sections: How does Dvorak do the the things he writes about in his article? How does he Other the various elements of music?

Things to think about:

  • The main English horn theme sounds like an imitation of an African-American spiritual. (So much so that it has since been given words and has become a spiritual, Goin' Home!)
  • Why would you use a Black spiritual to represent an Indian funeral lament?
  • Why would American middle- to upper-class audiences buy into this? Why does no one ever point out how strange this combination is, still?
  • Think about how different the situation for musical nationalism was (is?) in America than for Dvorak or Mussorgsky in Europe. First of all, up to this point the main goal of American music administrators and musicians had been to match the standard of European, especially German music (or at least the standard of the way the British matched German standards). The goal was to produce unmarked normal-style music, not something that sounded national. Partly this is a developing country trying to prove its worth on a world stage.
  • Another part, though, is that the US did not have the same concept of blood-and-soil, of Volk culture, that European countries had (more similar to the UK with its cosmopolitan empire but at least they had the monarchy and centuries of tradition). The country was, in concept at least, based on a political idea, not ethnic or cultural unity. (Though in practice we can recognize that America's ruling classes thought of themselves as part of a broader Anglo-Saxon Western Civilization.) So who was the Volk in the United States?
  • In other words, Dvorak's mishmash of references in the New World symphony might seem like offensive appropriation; but there is a way to read it as quite subversive in relation to American politics (though perhaps not intentionally so). Because Dvorak is making, through his article and much more forcefully through his music, the claim that the Volk of America are first and foremost, Native peoples and African-Americans.
  • At the same time, this was performed by all-white ensembles for all-white audiences. What is the difference between (1) including imitations of Indian or Black music in a symphony, (2) including actual Indian or Black music in a concert hall, (3) including actual Native American or African American people in the concert hall, (4) including them in the actual fabric of civil society?
  • How many attempts to create musical diversity even today actually paper over the reality of social segregation and inequality?
