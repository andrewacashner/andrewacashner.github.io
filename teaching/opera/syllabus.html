Learning Goals and Assessments

Course Description

A small number of representative operas will be used to highlight the history of this controversial 400-year old art form and its creators, performers, and audiences. Drama, music, staging, spectacle, and dance will be examined as components of production. Divas welcome.

Learning Goals

  1. (Knowledge) Acquire broad fundamental knowledge of the opera genre and a deeper knowledge of a handful of representative examples, including the major styles and traditions and their social and political aspects.
  2. (Perception, Analysis) Learn to hear how music shapes character and dramatic situations in various kinds of opera.
  3. (Analysis, Close reading) Understand how music relates to words, story, character, acting, and stagecraft in shaping the dramatic experience of opera.
  4. (Communication, Synthesis) Develop skills in research and writing, discussion, collaboration, and oral presentation.

Essays

Weekly response essays

Goal: Reflect critically on your own learning process, respond to what you are hearing and reading and identify research interests. Enable your professor to track with your learning, identify interests and concerns, need for further explanation or help, understand what motivates, excites, bothers you.

Write a short essay each week in which you reflect on what you learned in the previous week and respond to the reading and listening assignments for the coming week. This is not a research paper, and you may express yourself freely, but the writing should be clear and free from grammatical errors or sloppy mistakes. In prior experience, consistently good work in weekly essays correlates strongly with overall success in the course.

1–2 pages, about 650 words (12-pt. font, double spaced, 1-inch margins)

Due via Blackboard by the start of the first class session each week (typically Monday 9 a.m.)

Analytical essay

Goal: See second and third learning goals above.

Choose a complete scene from any opera studied in this class. Professor must approve your choice of scene. Write an essay in which you analyze how the libretto, score, vocal performance, staging (sets, costumes, movement), acting, and other relevant elements work together to produce a dramatic effect. The dramatic effect may be conveying a sense of character, conflict between characters, plot action, a reflective break from plot action, a sense of climax, and so on.

Every paper should include a comparison of two different recorded (audio or video) performances of the scene.

If you can read music, you are encouraged to analyze the notated music to the extent possible, and refer to the printed score in addition to any recordings you may consult. If not, base your analysis on a particular recording and refer to specific track timings. You do not need to use any technical musical vocabulary beyond what we have discussed in class, but you do need to engage with the sound of music in whatever way you can. Likewise you should analyze elements like lighting and dance even if you don’t know the technical vocabulary for them.

This paper should be the result of your own analytical work. You may not collaborate on any element of this project. You do not need to do any research in secondary sources (e.g., books about the opera) but as always you must cite any sources you do use.

5–6 pages, around 1600 words; due via Blackboard (see schedule for due date)

Group research presentation

Goal: See learning goals 1 and 4 above

Working with a group of other students, prepare and present an in-class presentation on an opera not covered in the course. Presentations should include the following elements:

  1. History of the opera: Source of the story, authorship of libretto, composition of score, first productions, reception (history of its performance and influence)
  2. Outline of the story, characters, and main themes
  3. Analysis of an excerpt of the music (such as an aria or ensemble) that illustrates characteristic features of how this opera works musically and dramatically, such as distinctive techniques, timbres, or dramatic strategies
  4. Discussion of at least one notable staging and how it highlights or undercuts the main challenges (musical, conceptual, political) of the work.
  5. An in-class presentation in which every group member participates. Presentation should be clear, well-prepared and organized, concise; it should raise and hold the interest of someone generally interested in opera
  6. An audiovisual element to the presentation, such as slides or a handout
  7. Bibliography of no less than five sources of secondary scholarship (like journal articles and monographs) about the opera or directly relevant to it

Coming to Class and Getting Involved

I will take attendance at every class, and the teaching assistant will take attendance at every workshop. You will learn best if you come to every class and get involved as fully as you can in whatever we are doing in the classroom. That involvement starts before you come to class as you prepare each class session’s listening and reading assignments, as you write your weekly assignment, and as you do your other coursework alone and with your classmates.

You can find the detailed listings, including links to online texts and music, by clicking on the heading for each class session on the schedule page. You are expected listen to and read all of the assigned material before you come to class that day.

Workshops are an important component of the class, in which you have the opportunity to work in smaller groups with the teaching assistant on a variety of tasks and topics. These are meant to be hands-on learning opportunities to build your skills and give you the chance to get help in learning; as well as allowing us to deal with some topics that don’t fit as well in the format of the rest of the class.

Academic Integrity

XXX

All assignments and activities associated with this course must be performed in accordance with the University of Rochester’s Academic Honesty Policy.

Academic honesty means avoiding anything that would unfairly advance your academic standing over that of your classmates, such as cheating on an exam or presenting someone else’s work as your own. Plagiarism includes any verbatim copying; unattributed, incorrectly attributed, false, or misleading citations; or unacknowledged help from others (e.g., having someone else write a paper for you).

Group assignments will include specific additional instructions about how to comply with the Academic Honesty Policy in your collaborative work. If you are not sure whether something counts as plagiarism or academic dishonesty, I can help you if you ask me about it before turning in the assignment.

Every instance of academic dishonesty will be reported to the College’s Board on Academic Honesty. The typical penalty for plagiarism on an assignment is a grade of zero in the assignment and a one-third reduction in the total letter grade for the course. Penalties for repeat or egregious offenses can extend to a grade of XE in the course or suspension from the university. Please note that plagiarism on a draft is typically treated as a separate infraction from plagiarism on a final revision of the same paper.
