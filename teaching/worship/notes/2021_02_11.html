1 Corinthians: Elements and theology of worship

  • Glimpse of a community in crisis, in transition

  • Eucharist - bread and wine as body and blood of Christ
  • community meal - agapes (“love feasts”)
    • one body (1 Cor 12:12-13)
    • presence of risen Christ

    • remembering the death of Christ

    • debate over meat sacrificed to idols

  • traditio - what is passed on (1 Cor 11:23)

  • Last Supper

  • emphasis on love: highest of spiritual gifts, summit of all Christian life
  • universal salvation – not just for the Jews
    • “Jews and Greeks” both
  • baptism - ritual/ceremonial washing
    • death and rebirth
    • cleansing from sin
    • entrance into community/family
    • “rites of Christian initiation” (plus anointing with oil)
  • spiritual gifts - prophecy, teaching, knowledge, “tongues”

  • glimpses of heaven

  • Paul: but if you don’t have love it doesn’t matter
    • cf. Jesus in John: “love one another as I have loved you”
    • cf. prophetic tradition of critiquing worship that is not accompanied by justice, as in Amos

Didache vs. Birkat ha-mazon

  • some obvious similarities, though the relationship is complex and hard to be certain of
  • Didache dates from possibly 50-75 AD; Birkat hamazon is an old tradition but the earliest written source is from several centuries later

Basic patterns of worship c. 2nd century

  • is not a single pattern of worship - there are many changing local traditions
    • example, at the beginning of the year (around winter solstice), themes of worship may have depended on which gospel a community had
      • e.g., Matthew starts with birth of Jesus and visitation of three kings, like the feast of Epiphany; while Mark starts with Jesus’s baptism and ministry and doesn’t include his birth, like the feast of Christ’s baptism in the same time of year
  • coalesce later into smaller number of traditions

Practices and concepts of music in the earliest stages

Kyrie eleison

  • One song whose words go all the way back: the Kyrie at opening of worship
  • Kyrie eleison - Lord have mercy, Christ have mercy, Lord have mercy
  • In Greek language, kept in Greek even after Roman Christians switched to Latin in 3rd-4th centuries – why?
    • possibly because they already knew it to certain tunes (like Christmas carols today that use thees and thous)
    • more likely (or in addition), because these were loaded words for Greek-speaking Christians, connecting back to the specific personal name of God in the Old Testament and his “steadfast love” or covenant love
    • Kyrios - same word used in the Greek translation of Old Testament (Septuagint) for divine name (YHWH)
    • eleos (mercy) - used in Septuagint for hesed - steadfast love, covenant love
