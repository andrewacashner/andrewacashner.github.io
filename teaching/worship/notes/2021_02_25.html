Worship in Jerusalem and Constantinople

  • stational worship
  • Egeria
  • “mysteriological piety”
    • mysterion (= sacrament) – e.g, Eucharist
    • devotion centered on awe-inspiring experiences
      • tension between objective and subjective views of worship
  • monasticism
    • first monk: St. Anthony of Egypt
      • Athanasius, Life of Anthony
      • radical discipleship - vows of poverty
      • in desert, hermetic - but people come to them
      • spiritual warriors, doing battle with the devil on behalf of everyone else
      • “inner martyrdom” – self-inflicted asceticism (denying self)
  • cathedral and monastic rites
    • cathedral: public, participatory, multisensory, attuned to time of day and season – commoners led by specialists
    • monastic: private (closed or isolated communities), ascetic, more bare-bones, more hard-core – specialists
  • strong monastic influence on cathedral worship in both East and West, increasing

Eastern Orthodoxy

  • conversion of Emperor Constantine, shifting capital of empire from Rome to Byzantium ( => Constantinople ( => Istanbul) )

  • SS. Cyril & Methodius bring Orthodoxy to Russia

  • organized in national churches with concept of spiritual union (acknowledge primacy of the patriarch of Constantinople)

  • worship in the vernacular (tends to be old-fashioned/archaic language)

  • “we did not know whether we were in heaven or on earth”
  • theosis – union with God, sanctification – ongoing work of the Holy Spirit to transform people together into a holy community
  • participation/represenation/anamnesis (remembering through doing)
    • “do this in remembrance of me”
    • physical embodiments, reenactments
    • actively drawing people into life of Christ
  • Liturgy of St. James – Jerusalem
    • “Oriental Orthodox”
  • Two main liturgies for Eastern Orthodoxy:
    • Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom
    • Liturgy of St. Basil the Great
