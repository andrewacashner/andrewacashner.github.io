Reactions, Characteristics

  • polyphonic setting

  • some parts monophonic = chant

  • [female and] male voices in choir (originally male voices)
  • choir of instruments:
    • organ
    • hornlike instruments:
      • sacabuches (sackbutts) – trombone
      • cornetts
      • trumpet (?)
  • alternatim practice – alternating between chant, polyphony

  • some parts instrumental only

  • calm
  • transports you to a heavenly setting

  • overlapping voices, words

Spanish music in Mexico

  • Hernando Franco (1532-1585)
    • born, trained in Spain
    • chapelmaster at Mexico City Cathedral
  • Is there anything “Mexican” about this?
    • does not seem to be any “folk” influence
    • conforms to norms of Spanish elevated church music
    • reflects limitations of what survives (primarily notated music in cathedral archives)

Compare: Franco, Sancta Maria e yn ilhuicac cihuapille (motet in Náhuatl)

- polyphonic - multiple voices in choir
- homorhythmic - all same rhythm 
- words clear, not overlapping

Concepts of intercultural exchange

  • Nueva España – New Spain
    • imposition of purified concept of Old World on the New
    • officially not mestizo culture (mestizaje) - mixed/mixture
      • but there was a lot of mixture
      • musically mixture happened more in folk/popular music in unwritten traditions
  • Ines Zúpanov, Missionary Tropics (about Jesuit missions in Asia)
    • tropics, tropical – same root as trope – “turning”
    • European culture/religion/practice, brought to “tropical” zone, inevitably changed into something else, unpredictably and inevitably
  • hybridization

  • accommodation – translation

  • “spaces of correlation” (Cécile Fromont)

  • incommensurability – what measures one thing, can’t be used to measure the other, no common denominator

Routes, sites of encounter, stages of European colonial expansion

  • 1453 – Fall of Constantinople

  • revival of Greek/Latin Classical learning -> Renaissance, Catholic Humanism

  • 1450s - Portuguese exploration of West & Central African coast, finding trade routes around the horn of Africa to Goa (India) and Macao (China)

  • conversion of Christian Kingdom of Kongo

  • Jesuit missions in India, China, Japan
    • “outer circle missions” (Gauvin Bailey)
      • Europeans outnumbered; accommodationist
      • -> “Chinese Rites Controversy”
  • 1492
    1. end of the Spanish reconquista under Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile - final conquest of the Moors (Granada)
    2. expulsion of the Jews
    3. send Columbus to the Indies via a westward route
  • Conquests of Indigenous American empires: Aztecs (Cortez), Incas (Pizarro)
    • (though lots of land and people are never really conquered)
  • Founding of Manila, establishment of global trade routes
    • From Seville/Lisbon to Manila via Angola and Goa
    • From Manila to Acapulco (Mexico)
    • From Veracruz (Mexico) to Seville

  • Trade in enslaved human beings first from central Africa to Bahía (Brazil) and other ports northward; later from west Africa

  • Spanish Empire in America - “inner-circle missions”
    • Spanish have control and act as colonizing powers
