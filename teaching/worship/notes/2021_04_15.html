Methodist movement

  • Outgrowth of the Church of England
    • Book of Common Prayer compiled, edited by Thomas Cranmer, archbishop of Canterbury
    • via media: “middle way” between Catholicism and Protestantism
    • founded on compromise
      • example: words spoken as the Eucharist is given out
"The body of Christ, (~Roman Catholic)
the bread of heaven; (~Catholic/Orthodox, ~Calvinist)
the blood of Christ, (~Catholic/Orthodox)
the cup of salvation: (~Lutheran)
take them in remembrance that Christ died for you (~Zwingli/Calvin/Anabaptist)
and feed on them spiritually in your hearts (~Calvinist)
by faith." (~Lutheran)
  • Reform movements within the Anglican church
    • Puritanism (more Calvinist!)
    • Methodism
      • Precursors: Lutheran Pietism, Bohemian Brethren
        • emphasis on personal emotion and commitment
        • meet in small groups for Bible study and prayer, mutual accountability
        • personal discipleship
        • personal relationship with Jesus
  • John Wesley - priest, reformer
    • Aldersgate conversion experience
    • “I felt my heart strangely warmed” during reading of Luther’s commentary on Galatians
    • “I had the faith of a servant, not a son” (reference to the Prodigal Son parable of Jesus)
    • founds “Methodist” societies
    • initially reform movement: emphasis on personal discipleship, on the ongoing work of the Holy Spirit in a believer’s life (continual conversion), care for the poor and evangelism
    • kicked out of the Church of England, become independent church
    • movement moves to America:
      • Francis Asbury, circuit-riding evangelist
    • advantages: adaptability/flexibility, esp. on frontier
  • Charles Wesley (brother) - poet, extremely prolific hymn writer
    • combination of intense personal devotion and intense theology
    • exx: “Hark the Herald Angels Sing”, “Come, Sinners to the Gospel Feast”
  • “Christianity for the common man”

Theological distinctives

  • work of the Holy Spirit
    • Arminian (as opposed to Calvinist) doctrine of salvation: grace is offered and can be refused or accepted
    • “God wants all people to saved”
    • types/stages of grace
      1. prevenient grace - goes before, prepares person for salvation
      2. justifying grace - saves you, forgives sins
      3. sanctifying grace - makes you holy
    • process conversion
  • call to holiness
    • “entire sanctification” - potential for God to bring a person to complete holiness
      • tendency toward legalism
        • no dancing, no drinking, no card playing, no gambling
    • vs. “backsliding”
  • sources of authority
    • “Wesleyan quadrilateral”
      1. Scripture
      2. Reason
      3. Tradition
      4. Experience

Evangelical movement/Revivalism

“Frontier worship” – combination of Methodist, Anabaptist, Presbyterian, etc. - revivalism: campmeeting evangelistic gatherings

Free Methodist movement - B. T. Roberts - against paying for pews: freedom from poverty and social inequality - against secret societies (Freemasons) - against social vices - for the ordination of women - abolitionism

from psalmody to hymnody

  • metrical, lined-out psalmody
  • Isaac Watts, The Psalms of David Imitated in the Language of the New Testament
    • ex. “Joy to the World” (Psalm 98)
    • Christianizing the psalter
    • opens the door for free-composed new hymns
    • personal emotional response to Jesus

  • Charles Wesley = successor

  • “heart religion” -> religion of the hands
