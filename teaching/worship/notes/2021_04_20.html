Frontier worship, campmeeting revivalism, and the evangelical movement

Gospel hymns

  • Watts, Wesley, Moody & Sankey, Fanny Crosby and others

  • campmeetings on the frontier
    • eclectic gatherings, denominational distinctives get blurred
    • itinerant evangelists (preachers)
    • centered on making a personal commitment to Jesus
      • “altar call”
      • highly emotional, personal
      • group enthusiasm
    • (mid-later 19th c.) borrowing from secular media, production
  • evangelicalism is a style of Christianity
    • emphasis on personal conversion and commitment
      • emotion (“heart religion”)
      • personal relationship to Jesus
      • Bible as authority, as a personal tool for spirituality
        • “Jesus loves me, this I know, ’cause the Bible tells me so”
    • de-emphasis on the church, institutions, sacraments
    • de-emphasis on social justice, corporate identity
      • except in “social gospel” movement which tried to remedy this problem
  • flexibility of use: different tunes, adding choruses (refrains)
  • play the tunes in very different styles

  • used in Protestant mission work/translation

Examples

  • Isaac Watts
    • increasingly free versions of Psalm 1
    • “Our God, Our Help in Ages Past”
    • “Joy to the World, the Lord Is Come”
    • “When I Survey the Wondrous Cross”
  • Charles Wesley
    • “O, For a Thousand Tongues to Sing”
    • “Come ye that love the Lord”
      • later version with added chorus: “We’re Marching to Zion”
  • Gospel Hymns (hymnal)
    • “I Need Thee Every Hour”

Fundamentalism

  • reaction to Enlightenment modernism, science, secularization of civic life
    • theory of evolution
    • historical/critical Biblical scholarship
    • changing social mores, including racial integration and increased rights for women
  • “The Fundamentals”
    • the divine inspiration of Scripture
    • the virgin birth of Jesus
    • literal 6-day creation
    • etc.

  • emphasis on certainty, being right

  • originally a distinct movement, now a style or tendency within Evangelical Christianity (with analogous movements within Catholicism and elsewhere)

  • lack of specificity in evangelical hymns allows people to bring in a lot of other elements like allegiance to a political party without challenge
