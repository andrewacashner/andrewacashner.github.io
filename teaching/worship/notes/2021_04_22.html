Outgrowths of the Evangelical Movement and its Musical Traditions

  • personal, emotional spirituality without a strong corporate dimension

  • vs. abolitionists, concern for social welfare
    • “social gospel” movement - e.g., Salvation Army
  • split between sacred and secular; between worship and work/service; in the temple vs. out in the world
    • liberal (esp. mainline churches) vs. fundamentalism
    • theological liberalism
      • demystification
      • demythologizing
      • emphasis on moral teachings
      • science over faith
    • see Nancey Murphy, Beyond Liberalism and Fundamentalism and Anglo-American Postmodernity – two sides of same coin, philosophical “foundationalism”
  • Charismatic movement (Greek charism, gift of grace)
    • international, sudden reawakening/revival, e.g., in Korea
    • strong emphasis on the supernatural, on the “gifts of the Spirit”
      • speaking in tongues, miraculous healing, prophecy, etc.
  • The Black Church (not a monolith!)
    • musical/worship practices developed during slavery era
      • Dr. Watts singing (based on lined-out psalmody)
        • an outgrowth of “Old Way of Singing”
      • ring shouts
      • moans
      • spirituals
        • connecting to ancient Hebrew practices of lament, as in psalms, book of Lamentations
      • part of blend of traditions that contribute to rise of blues, jazz
      • fusion of these traditions with blues, jazz -> Black gospel
    • speech/song continuum (Wm. T. Dargan, Lining Out the Word)
    • structure/improvisation continuum
  • shape-note movement
    • Singing schools, teaching music notation with a simplified system with different note shapes
    • trying to clean up “Old Way of Singing”, teach notation
    • but actually ends up preserving many aspects of it, in vocal style, “primitive” harmonies
  • Oneida hymn singers
    • Oneida nation, one of Six Nations of the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy indigenous to New York State
    • now most Oneida live on reservation in Wisconsin
    • living oral tradition of hymn-singing in Oneida language; musical aspects seem to be a fusion of white European psalm and hymn singing traditions, singing-school/shape-note type singing, and Native traditions of vocalism

  • consider, why did African American and Native American people become Christians? how was their Christianity different from that of Euro-Americans?
